# ValleyTech Custom Solutions: A Pattern of Exploitation

I did not want to have to make this repository. I am not a drama chaser and I do not get satisfaction from calling people out. But when a 15-year-old kid gets used as a pawn, loses money, and then gets blamed for it -- while the person who set him up continues to play the victim and the community blackballs anyone who speaks up -- I cannot stay quiet.

My name does not matter. What matters is that I stood up for Illya, the kid behind Arch Labs, and for that I got blackballed from the cybersecurity hardware community. ValleyTech Custom Solutions continues to push their narrative while evidence of what actually happened sits in plain view. This repository is my attempt to make sure the full story stays available for anyone who wants to see it.

I stand with Illya. I stand with the truth. And I am tired of watching people get run out of the community for telling it.

---

## What Happened

The story is straightforward. It is documented in video, in screenshots, and in emails. There is nothing to interpret.

**The RF Reaper Incident**

In January 2026, Khalil (the owner of ValleyTech Custom Solutions) reached out to Illya, a 15-year-old who runs a small electronics store called Arch Labs. Khalil sent Illya a private link to pre-order a device called the RF Reaper -- an all-in-one cybersecurity tool with ESP32, CC1101, NRF24, GPS, NFC, and more.

Khalil told Illya to buy 10 units. He said it was the smartest thing Illya could do. He said the Bruce Dev Team was broke and could not afford to manufacture them. He said they would sell for $250 and sell out fast on Illya's store.

Illya trusted him. He spent $865 on the order, plus $60 shipping and $130 tariffs -- over $1,000 total for a 15-year-old kid.

What arrived was not what was advertised. The boards came as bare PCBs: no battery, no GPS module, no CC1101 radio, no screen, no antennas, no SMA connectors. The single most expensive component on the board -- the CC1101 module -- was not included.

Elecrow offered Illya a $75 refund. Seventy-five dollars on an order that cost well over a thousand. That would not even cover one CC1101 module.

Illya had to spend his own money to finish the boards he already paid for. Each Reaper ended up costing him $110 in parts alone, not counting his labor. Meanwhile, ValleyTech was out promoting the same device for $80 from their own store.

And when Illya spoke up about it, the community turned on him. Not on Khalil. Not on ValleyTech. On the kid.

## The Pattern

This is not about one bad deal. It is about a pattern of behavior:

1. **Targeting younger or less experienced people** -- Illya was 15. He was brought into the community by someone Khalil knew. He trusted the authority figure who told him he was like a brother.

2. **Shifting risk onto others** -- Khalil could not afford to buy the boards himself, so he had Illya buy them. Illya took the financial risk. Illya took the customer complaints. Khalil took none of it.

3. **Changing the story when things go wrong** -- Promised one price, charged another. Promised a complete product, received bare boards. Then suddenly Illya "was never supposed to buy 10" and "was never supposed to have the link."

4. **Leaking closed-source files then blaming the recipient** -- Khalil gave Illya the full design files for the RF Reaper -- Gerbers, BOM, STL files -- files that were supposed to be closed source. Then Illya got in trouble for having them.

5. **Undercutting the person you set up** -- After having Illya buy 10 units at $65 each and list them at $250, ValleyTech promoted the Bruce Dev Team's version of the same device for $80.

6. **Continuing to push the narrative** -- ValleyTech still has 96,000 subscribers. They still make videos. They still sell products. Meanwhile, anyone who brings up what happened gets pushed out of the community.
